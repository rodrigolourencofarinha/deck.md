## Core contract

A finished consulting slide is a contract with the reader:

- The title is the conclusion.
- The subheading is the orientation.
- The body is the proof.
- The footer is the receipt.

Golden rule:
- one slide, one insight
- the insight is fully stated in the title
- the body proves that insight
- nothing in the title is unsupported by the body
- nothing in the body is unrelated to the title

If a slide has two insights, split it.
If the title cannot be written as one clear sentence, the slide logic is not ready.

## Deck-first thinking

Before production, define:
- audience
- objective
- decision, action, or understanding the deck must create
- whether the deck is read alone or presented live

Use a storyline before slides:
- SCR or SCQA: Situation, Complication, Question, Answer
- Problem, Analysis, Solution
- Current state, Opportunity or Risk, Recommendation

Draft the action-title sequence before building bodies.
Reading the titles alone should tell the deck's full argument.

Density rule:
- live presentation: lower density, cleaner teaching flow, presenter carries detail
- read-alone pack: more evidence per page, but hierarchy must stay aggressive

## Action titles

An action title is a short complete sentence that states the slide's takeaway.
It is not a topic label.

Rules:
- state the conclusion, not the process
- keep it ideally under 15 words
- one line is ideal; two lines is the practical maximum
- include the key number, entity, or comparison when it matters
- use active voice and simple words
- keep voice, tense, and length consistent across the deck

Bad:
- Market overview
- Historical development in revenue and costs
- Recommendations

Better:
- US luxury growth has been concentrated in the top three players
- Costs have grown twice as fast as revenue over five years
- Three initiatives can close the 2026 EBITDA gap

## Slide anatomy

Default content-slide structure:

1. Action title: the insight.
2. Subheading or kicker: what the reader is looking at, including scope, unit, geography, period, or sample.
3. Body: chart, table, framework, diagram, or structured text proving the title.
4. Optional takeaway or implication box: reinforces the title, never introduces a new message.
5. Footer: source, notes, page number, date, and status marking when needed.

Subheadings should orient the reader.
They should not repeat the title or introduce a second insight.

Good subheading:
- Sales of personal luxury goods, US market, $ billions, 2015-2024

Weak subheading:
- Important chart
- This shows costs are rising
- Figure 3

## Body as proof

The body has one job: make the title feel inevitable.

Rules:
- every chart, bullet, label, icon, and annotation must support the title
- use the simplest visual that proves the point
- prefer structure over prose
- group qualitative points into MECE categories
- use 2-5 strong support points when possible
- use 5-7 items as a practical upper bound for one grouping
- make the most important point the largest, boldest, most central, or accented
- use one accent color for the key point and grayscale for context
- delete interesting but off-message details

If the slide needs a paragraph to explain the chart, pick a simpler chart or split the slide.

## Content type choices

Use the simplest form that matches the evidence:

- Trend over time: line chart
- Category comparison: bar chart
- Breakdown at a point in time: bar chart or table; use pie sparingly
- Part-to-whole across categories: stacked bar or Mekko
- Relationship between variables: scatter plot
- Cumulative movement: waterfall
- Structured qualitative comparison: 2x2, matrix, or table
- Sequence: numbered process, roadmap, or arrow flow
- Hierarchy: pyramid, tree, or org chart
- Recommendation: numbered actions grouped by MECE categories
- Risk: risk and mitigation together, often likelihood by impact
- Next steps: action verbs plus owner and date where possible

Quantitative slide:
- chart should dominate the body
- title states the quantitative insight
- subheading states what, unit, and scope
- one accent color highlights the point
- source is mandatory

Qualitative slide:
- structure beats prose
- use boxes, modules, matrices, or frameworks
- avoid paragraph blocks

## Footer and source hygiene

Every data slide needs a footer with:
- source
- notes or footnotes for caveats
- page number
- date when useful
- status sticker when needed

Never leave data unsourced.
Never leave a number without a unit.
Use status stickers only when the reader needs the warning, such as:
- Preliminary
- Illustrative
- Indicative
- For discussion
- Draft

## Horizontal and vertical flow

Vertical flow:
- title, subheading, body, callouts, and footer reinforce the same message
- body proves the title with no orphan details

Horizontal flow:
- each action title connects logically to the prior and next slide
- the title sequence alone tells the argument

If a slide works internally but breaks the storyline, move it, rewrite it, or cut it.
If it fits the story but the body does not prove the title, rebuild the body.

## Build sequence

For one slide:

1. Clarify audience and objective.
2. State the one insight as a plain sentence.
3. Identify the minimum evidence that would convince a skeptic quickly.
4. Pick the simplest visual form.
5. Draft the subheading with scope and units.
6. Lay out the body with visual hierarchy.
7. Write the final action title.
8. Add source, notes, page number, date, and status marking as needed.
9. Prune anything that does not support the title.
10. Review the rendered slide like a busy executive.

For a deck:

1. Define objective and audience.
2. Choose read-alone or live-presentation density.
3. Draft the storyline as SCR, SCQA, or another clear argument chain.
4. Draft all action titles.
5. Read titles end to end and fix the story before production.
6. Set style, template, title placement, footer behavior, and chart conventions.
7. Build slide bodies.
8. Polish alignment, spacing, sources, units, and consistency.

## Pre-flight checklist

Before calling a slide done:

- The title is a complete sentence, not a noun phrase.
- The title states the insight, not the topic.
- The title fits in at most two lines.
- The body proves the title and nothing else.
- The most important element is visually dominant.
- The visual form is the simplest one that carries the message.
- Numbers have units.
- Charts have clear labels and direct annotations where helpful.
- Source and footnotes are present where needed.
- The slide fits the deck storyline.
- The style, title position, colors, and footer match the rest of the deck.
- A busy reader can understand the point in a few seconds.

## Anti-patterns

Avoid:
- topic-label titles such as "Market overview"
- wall-of-text bodies
- kitchen-sink slides that preserve every analysis
- shrinking fonts to avoid splitting content
- unsourced statistics
- decorative charts, 3D effects, heavy shadows, and gratuitous gradients
- redundant legends when direct labels work
- five colors when two are enough
- two insights on one slide
- charts that require a paragraph to decode
- inconsistent fonts, colors, title placement, or footers
